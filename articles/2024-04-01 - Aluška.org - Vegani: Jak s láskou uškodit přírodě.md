## Sumarizace relevantnosti

Článek kriticky zkoumá ekologické dopady veganství a tvrdí, že má vlastně větší environmentální dopad než tradiční živočišné zemědělství. Veganský rámec je dominantní (veganství je hlavním tématem), a environmentální rámec je rovněž dominantní (diskuse o emisích, cyklování živin, dopadech na půdu). Článek argumentuje pro tradičnější chov zvířat a rybolov jako ekologičtější alternativy. Splňuje obě kritéria.

## Text článku

This Czech article argues that veganism may not be environmentally beneficial, contrary to popular belief.

## Key Arguments

**Land Use Efficiency**: The author claims animal agriculture from aquaculture (fish farming) can be as productive as plant-based farming. Fish farms yield "100 times more" per hectare than field crops, requiring "5-10 times less energy" since fish are cold-blooded.

**Phosphorus Cycling**: Large herbivores are essential for soil health. Their waste rapidly returns phosphorus to soil—without them, "soil loses 98% of phosphorus input." Australia lost megafauna 50,000 years ago and now has severely depleted soils dominated by fire-adapted eucalyptus.

**Emissions Reality**: The piece notes that while livestock produces higher emissions, fish farming produces similar CO2 to fruits and berries (around 2.2-2.5 kg per kilogram), and satisfies hunger faster, potentially lowering total consumption.

## Contested Points

The article criticizes environmentalists for preventing wild large herbivores' return while supporting vegan diets, suggesting ideological rather than scientific motivations. It suggests "biomass increases with higher CO2 emissions"—a controversial claim.

**Bottom line**: The author contends rational animal agriculture may harm ecosystems less than replacing livestock with plant farming alone.

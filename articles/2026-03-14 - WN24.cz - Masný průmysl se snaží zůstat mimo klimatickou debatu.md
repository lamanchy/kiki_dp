## Sumarizace relevantnosti

Článek dokumentuje lobbing masného a mlékárenského průmyslu, který se snaží zůstat mimo hlavní klimatickou debatu, přestože sektor produkuje zhruba jednu třetinu globálních emisí skleníkových plynů. Environmentální rámec (GHG emise, lobbying v klimatických jednáních, role masného průmyslu v klimatické krizi) tvoří páteř textu. Veganský rámec je přítomen jako řešení (přechod na rostlinné stravy, dánské iniciativy na podporu rostlinných potravin).

## Text článku

The meat and dairy industry attempts to remain outside primary climate discussions, according to a report from the EAT-Lancet Commission. Food and agriculture sectors account for roughly one-third of global greenhouse gas emissions worldwide.

Research indicates that transitioning to predominantly plant-based diets could reduce agricultural emissions by approximately 15 percent and potentially save around 15 million lives annually. Approximately 60 percent of Earth's mammals consist of livestock, including cattle that produce methane, according to Greenpeace. Wild animals represent only four percent of mammals.

Plant-based eating grows increasingly popular, particularly among younger demographics, yet significant dietary shifts remain limited. Meat and dairy products frequently escape climate debate scrutiny due to hidden partnerships and communication strategies emphasizing their purported indispensability.

The meat and dairy sectors employ robust lobbying efforts. For example, brothers Joesley and Wesley Batista, owners of JBS (the world's largest beef producer), attended COP30 in Belém despite facing longstanding deforestation criticism. Brazil's government granted them VIP access despite commitments against forest destruction.

The UN's Food and Agriculture Organization faces accusations of bias favoring livestock production. Thanawat Tiensin, FAO's animal production division director, stated: "When my stomach is empty, I have no time to discuss sustainability," suggesting meat consumption will thrive.

Despite significant industry pressure, some nations modify food policy. Denmark launched a plant-food support initiative in 2023.
